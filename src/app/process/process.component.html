When I get a new project to work on, I usually follow a well-established process that allows me to understand exactly what the client needs and what concrete steps I should take in order to deliver the application on time.

Collecting requirements and agreeing on technologies and delivery
This is when we discuss your project idea and figure out exactly what you need to get done. I will ask you about the types of users the application will have and what it should do, how it should behave. At first I need to have a general view of it, but after that I will ask about specific details. We will also talk about which technologies you prefer and whether you would like me to:

  • Deliver the application in increments (some functionality is done first, I show it to you, I get your feedback then I continue to build upon this skeleton), OR:
  • Deliver the application all at once, and I only get your feedback in the end, so I won't bother you much during the process, but amendments will be harder to make in the end

I personally prefer the first way to do it, as I like to interact with the client during the development process.

Extracting user stories
After our discussion, I extract use-cases, or user stories for the application, from what you told me. These user stories are in the form of "As a [user type] I want to be able to [do something in the application] in order to [accomplish something]".

Splitting stories into tasks
Next, I break down these large use-cases into smaller parts according to technical criteria (for example, building the functionality on the back-end, connecting it to the front-end, making it look pretty and user-friendly, making unit and integration tests etc). If one of these tasks is too big I might need to split it into subtasks, but that is not always the case.

Task prioritizing and actual development
The last step before beginning to develop your application is prioritizing the tasks, so that I do the most important features first. After that, I begin to actually do the tasks.
